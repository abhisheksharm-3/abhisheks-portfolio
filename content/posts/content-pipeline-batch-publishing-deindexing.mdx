We published a content pipeline to a new domain in late April. The pipeline generates SEO articles from keyword clusters, validates quality, and publishes via GitHub pull requests to a Next.js site. On April 24 we pushed the first batch to production: 249 articles in a single day across 5 commits. Google indexed all of them within 48 hours.

Two weeks later, 247 of the 249 were gone.

I spent a day investigating with another Claude session that had access to the Git history and Search Console data. Here is what we found.

## What the data showed

Google Search Console showed 247 pages in "Crawled — currently not indexed" state. No manual actions, no penalties. The pages were accessible, returned 200, and passed the URL inspection tool's eligibility check. Google had crawled them, evaluated them, and chosen not to index them.

The 2 that stayed indexed were from the April 24 batch. Both happened to be on topics with high independent search demand. The other 247 were not penalised — they were algorithmically pruned.

The GSC chart showed the full shape: impressions peaked at 4,222 per day on April 27 when all 249 were indexed. By May 20 it had dropped to 509. By June 1 it was 260. The 94% drop in impressions is directly explained by going from 249 indexed pages to 2.

## Cause 1: 249 articles in one day

The Git history made this unambiguous:

| Commit | Articles added |
|---|---|
| "Add 20 articles: TCO, pricing, strategy guides" | 20 |
| "Articles registry: add 6 new strategy articles" | 6 |
| "Add 28 articles: strategy, compliance, security" | 28 |
| "Add 50 articles, updated case studies" | 50 |
| "Add 144 new articles across 13 content clusters" | 144 |
| "Add on-device AI education article" | 1 |
| **Total** | **249** |

All 5 commits landed on April 24. Google discovered and crawled them April 25-26: a new domain that had published 249 articles overnight.

Google's helpful content documentation directly addresses this: "mass-produced content by automation" is listed as a quality signal. 144 articles in a single commit is unambiguous on that dimension.

## Cause 2: CTR during the honeymoon window

Google gives new pages a trial window, roughly 7-10 days, where it shows them in search results to measure whether users click. CTR during this window determines which pages survive.

The GSC data for the non-indexed pages during April 28 to May 5:

| Page | Impressions | Clicks | CTR |
|---|---|---|---|
| native-offline-vs-pwa-field-teams | 2,059 | 1 | 0.05% |
| on-device-ai-vs-cloud-api | 958 | 1 | 0.10% |
| mobile-apps-frontline-workers | 591 | 1 | 0.17% |
| app-store-rejection-prevention | 551 | 1 | 0.18% |

Normal CTR at position 6-7 is around 3%. These pages were getting 0.05-0.18%, roughly 20-60x below normal. Google showed them to thousands of people, almost nobody clicked, and that engagement signal drove the evaluation.

The pages that stayed indexed had CTRs of 1-5% during the same window.

The titles and meta descriptions were the proximate cause of low CTR. Titles like "Mobile App Development for US Construction Companies: Field Apps, Safety Compliance, and AI 2026" read like a vendor brochure to someone searching "construction field service app". They described the article's contents rather than stating something useful the reader would get from clicking.

## Cause 3: cluster pruning

The most striking finding was from the correlation table we built by joining the Git commit history against GSC's index status.

| Cluster | Indexed | Total | Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Offline / Connectivity | 2 | 5 | 40% |
| US Industry Verticals | 7 | 23 | 30% |
| Agency Selection / Outsourcing | 12 | 48 | 25% |
| Vendor / Team Management | 8 | 36 | 22% |
| AI Features / Strategy | 3 | 14 | 21% |
| On-Device AI | 3 | 15 | 20% |
| Tech Stack / Framework | 5 | 27 | 18% |
| Cost / Pricing / ROI | 4 | 29 | 13% |
| Field Service / Logistics | 2 | 17 | 11% |
| Compliance and Security | 1 | 24 | 4% |

Every cluster had the same pattern. Google indexed 1-3 articles per cluster and dropped the rest. The survival rate correlated with search demand: fintech, healthcare, and logistics survived in the US industry cluster. Construction, agriculture, BPO, and hospitality did not.

The compliance cluster is the starkest: 24 articles published simultaneously, 1 survived (HIPAA). FERPA, FedRAMP, financial services compliance, insurance compliance. all dropped. Google picked the query with the highest search volume, indexed that article, and treated the other 23 as redundant coverage.

<PostCallout>
This is not a quality problem. Several of the dropped compliance articles were well-researched and substantively correct. Google did not evaluate their individual quality. It evaluated the cluster as a whole, picked a winner, and pruned the rest.
</PostCallout>

## Cause 4: date manipulation while google was crawling

The Git history revealed something we had not initially looked for: the publication dates were changed 4 times in the 48 hours after publishing, while Google was actively crawling.

| Date | Commit | What changed |
|---|---|---|
| Apr 25 | f9c9bd0 | All dates pushed back to Feb 2025 |
| Apr 25 | c9141e2 | Same day: dates rolled back to Jan 2026 |
| Apr 26 | 95b275b | Dates changed to Oct 2025 spread |
| May 1 | b0334f1 | New batch: all backdated to 2025-2026 |

The intent was to make the content library look like an established site with a publishing history, not a site that had launched 249 articles overnight. The execution made things worse.

Google crawls a page and stores the publication date from the article JSON-LD. When it re-crawls the same page 24 hours later and sees a different date, it stores that mismatch. When it sees 249 pages all changing their dates in the same direction on the same day, the pattern is detectable at the domain level.

Google's helpful content guidelines list "manipulating freshness dates artificially" as a quality signal. The commit history shows this happened to the entire batch within 48 hours of the initial crawl.

The confidence on this one is lower than the others. date changes alone are common and do not reliably cause deindexing. But combined with the other 3 causes, it removed the benefit of the doubt that a new domain needs.

## The control that confirmed it

The May 4-6 publishing run published 14 articles. No date manipulation. Small batch. Same content pipeline, same article structure, same domain.

13 of 14 indexed. 93% indexing rate, versus 0.8% (2/249) for the April batch.

The content is comparable. The domain authority did not meaningfully change between April 24 and May 4. The only differences are batch size and the absence of date manipulation. The indexing rate difference is the result.

The May articles that published after May 15, including ones on similar topic clusters to the dropped April articles, are also holding in the index. Same topics, different publishing cadence. indexed and stable.

## What actually fixes it

**For future publishing:** one article per cluster per run. Publish it, let it index, let it accumulate some CTR signal, then publish the next article in that cluster. When Google evaluates the second article, the first is already established and they are treated as complementary rather than competing. The cluster pruning does not happen when Google has time to evaluate each article independently.

**For the 247 dropped articles:** the ones that are genuinely unique topics are recoverable through GSC URL Inspection → Request Indexing, batched at 10-15 per day. The ones that are near-duplicate cluster variations are harder. For those, the path is either making them meaningfully different from the indexed version (unique data, different angle, different intent match) or consolidating them into the indexed article with 301 redirects.

**For the domain authority gap:** the site has no external backlinks from outside its own ecosystem. Internal links from indexed pages help but the authority dilutes across too many hops to reach deep articles. Even 5 relevant external backlinks to the domain would change the indexing dynamics significantly, based on the pattern we saw in the data and confirmed by multiple SEO community reports of the same experience.

The CTR issue is the most directly fixable right now. Rewriting titles to match actual search queries. not describing the article but stating the specific thing the reader gets. is what determines whether pages survive the next honeymoon window.
