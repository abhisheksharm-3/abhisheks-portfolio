I inherited an ACH payments feature that had been built and then disabled. The immediate business need was supporting bank debits for subscriptions over $5,000 per month, where a 2.9% card fee is real money and customers prefer ACH anyway. The previous developer had wired most of the Stripe integration and left it dormant, and nobody could confirm whether it was production-ready. The first day was an audit: which code paths were live, which were scaffolding, and which assumptions about the payment lifecycle were wrong.

The wrong assumption was the important one. The dormant code treated ACH like a card: confirm the payment, wait for success, grant access. That works for cards because confirmation and settlement happen in the same second. For ACH it produces a subscription that a customer has paid for but cannot use for the better part of a week. Fixing that meant rebuilding the provisioning logic around the part of the payment lifecycle that ACH actually has and cards do not: a multi-day gap between submitting a debit and knowing whether it cleared.

## What ACH changes about billing

Stripe's `us_bank_account` payment method is ACH Direct Debit. When you confirm a PaymentIntent against a card, it moves to `succeeded` almost immediately, and your webhook handler can safely treat `payment_intent.succeeded` as the moment to grant access. ACH does not work that way. Confirming an ACH debit moves the PaymentIntent into the `processing` state, which means the debit has been submitted to the customer's bank but has not cleared. Settlement takes four to five business days. Only then does the PaymentIntent transition to `succeeded`, or to a failed state if the debit is returned.

This single difference cascades through the whole subscription lifecycle. The first invoice's payment does not settle at checkout time, so a subscription created with the default payment behavior would sit in an incomplete state waiting for money that is days away. Renewal payments fail days after the renewal date rather than on it. A failed payment is not a card decline you can surface in the checkout UI; it is an asynchronous event that arrives long after the customer has left the page. Every piece of logic that assumed payment confirmation was synchronous had to be re-examined against a timeline measured in days.

## Provision on processing, not succeeded

The core decision was to provision access optimistically when the PaymentIntent enters `processing`, rather than waiting for `succeeded`. ACH has a high eventual success rate, and for a high-value managed tier, making a paying customer wait five days for access is a worse outcome than the rare case of provisioning someone whose debit later fails. The failure becomes a recovery problem handled after the fact, not a gate on access.

That means the webhook handler keys provisioning on `payment_intent.processing` and treats `payment_intent.succeeded` as confirmation rather than activation:

```typescript
// stripe-webhook/handlers.ts

async function handlePaymentIntentProcessing(pi: Stripe.PaymentIntent) {
  // ACH debit submitted, not yet cleared. Provision now.
  if (pi.payment_method_types.includes("us_bank_account")) {
    const sub = await resolveSubscription(pi);
    await provisionAccess(sub, { settlementPending: true });
  }
}

async function handlePaymentIntentSucceeded(pi: Stripe.PaymentIntent) {
  // Funds cleared. Confirm provisioning, clear the pending flag.
  const sub = await resolveSubscription(pi);
  await confirmSettlement(sub);
}
```

The `settlementPending` flag matters for reconciliation. Access is live, but the record carries the knowledge that the money has not actually arrived yet, so reporting and downstream logic can distinguish a confirmed-paid account from a provisioned-but-pending one. When `payment_intent.succeeded` arrives days later, `confirmSettlement` clears the flag and the account becomes a normal paid subscription.

For the subscription to stay active while the first payment is still processing rather than dropping into an incomplete state, the creation flow uses a payment behavior suited to delayed-notification methods. Stripe's payment behavior settings control exactly this: whether a subscription whose payment has not yet succeeded is treated as active or held incomplete. Choosing the behavior that keeps the subscription active during processing is what makes optimistic provisioning coherent with Stripe's own view of the subscription state, rather than fighting it.

## The grace period for failures

The failure case is where the design earns its complexity. An ACH debit can fail days after submission, most commonly for insufficient funds. By default, Stripe's dunning runs a retry schedule and can eventually cancel the subscription or mark it `unpaid`. For these accounts, automatic cancellation is the wrong behavior. A failed debit on a $5,000-plus subscription is a relationship event that a human should handle, not a trigger for silently tearing down access.

The business rule, confirmed with stakeholders, was that a failed ACH payment should never immediately suspend the customer. Instead they enter a two-month grace window during which they keep full access while recovery is handled manually. Implementing that meant taking the cancellation decision away from Stripe's automatic dunning and moving it into our own code. Stripe's retry settings were configured to leave an exhausted subscription in `past_due` rather than canceling it, so Stripe stops trying but does not tear anything down.

The grace logic itself is three pieces: a marker written on entry, webhook-driven restoration, and a cron-driven suspension at expiry.

```typescript
// On invoice.payment_failed (after Stripe's retries are exhausted)
async function handlePaymentFailed(invoice: Stripe.Invoice) {
  const sub = await resolveSubscription(invoice);
  if (!sub.graceExpiresAt) {
    await enterGrace(sub, {
      graceExpiresAt: addDays(now(), 60),
      reason: "ach_payment_failed",
    });
    await notifyOps(sub); // sales/ops handle recovery manually
  }
}

// On invoice.paid (customer or ops resolves the payment)
async function handleInvoicePaid(invoice: Stripe.Invoice) {
  const sub = await resolveSubscription(invoice);
  if (sub.graceExpiresAt) {
    await exitGrace(sub); // clear marker, restore normal state
  }
}
```

The marker is written once. The `if (!sub.graceExpiresAt)` guard means a second failure event inside an existing grace window does not extend or reset the clock, which keeps webhook replays and Stripe's own retry events from corrupting the deadline. Restoration is driven by `invoice.paid`: when the customer's bank account is funded and the retry succeeds, or when ops collects payment another way, the grace marker is cleared and the subscription returns to normal. Nothing suspends access on the failure event itself.

Suspension is a separate cron job that runs daily and acts only on subscriptions whose grace window has actually expired:

```typescript
// suspend-expired-grace (daily cron)
const expired = await db.subscriptions.where({
  graceExpiresAt: { lt: now() },
  status: "past_due",
});

for (const sub of expired) {
  await suspendAccess(sub);
  await cancelStripeSubscription(sub.stripeId);
}
```

Splitting the decision this way is deliberate. The failure webhook only ever opens a window. The cron is the only thing that closes it. That separation made the behavior far easier to reason about during testing, because suspension is not tangled up in the timing of asynchronous Stripe events.

## The entitlement guard

The premium tier grants a fixed allotment of editing credits per annual term. The dormant code allocated those credits on subscription creation, which is correct exactly once. The bug surfaced when a customer cancels and re-subscribes inside the same annual term: a new subscription object is created, the creation handler fires again, and the customer receives a second full annual allotment they did not pay for.

The root cause is keying entitlement on the wrong thing. The number of Stripe subscription objects a customer churns through is unrelated to how many annual allotments they are owed. Keying credit allocation on the subscription creation event conflates the two. The fix was a 360-day entitlement guard that keys allocation on the billing term instead:

```typescript
async function allocateAnnualCredits(customerId: string, sub: Subscription) {
  const recent = await db.creditGrants.findFirst({
    where: {
      customerId,
      type: "annual_allotment",
      grantedAt: { gt: subDays(now(), 360) },
    },
  });

  if (recent) {
    log.info("annual allotment already granted in current term, skipping", {
      customerId,
      previousGrant: recent.id,
    });
    return;
  }

  await db.creditGrants.create({ customerId, type: "annual_allotment", sub });
}
```

A re-subscription inside the window finds the prior grant and skips. A genuine renewal after the term finds nothing within 360 days and allocates correctly. The same idempotency principle, keying on the invoice rather than the event delivery, protects against webhook replays granting credits twice for a single legitimate payment. Stripe delivers webhooks at least once, not exactly once, so any handler that mutates entitlement has to be idempotent on something stable, never on the fact that an event arrived.

## The payment method leak

During production validation I found that ACH was being offered as a payment option to customers who were not on the premium tier. ACH was meant to be available only on the high-value checkout flow, and it was showing up everywhere. The cause was Stripe payment method configuration inheritance: enabling `us_bank_account` at the account's default configuration level meant it propagated to checkout sessions that were supposed to be card-only.

The fix was to stop relying on the default configuration and instead scope payment methods explicitly per checkout flow, so ACH is enabled only on the premium path and the standard path stays card-only. This is the kind of issue that does not appear in any code path you would test in isolation, because the leak lives in account-level configuration rather than in the checkout code itself. It only showed up by actually walking the non-premium checkout in production and noticing an option that should not have been there.

## Testing with test clocks

None of the grace-period behavior can be validated on a normal development timeline, because the events that matter are days apart. Stripe Test Clocks solve this. You bind a test customer and subscription to a simulated clock and advance time programmatically, fast-forwarding through the ACH settlement window, the grace period, and renewal cycles in seconds.

The scenarios I ran through Test Clocks covered the full lifecycle for both individual and team accounts: a debit entering `processing` and being provisioned, the clock advanced to settlement with a forced success, then a separate run with a forced failure to verify grace entry, then advancing the clock past 60 days to confirm the suspension cron fired exactly once at expiry. I also tested re-subscription inside the term against the entitlement guard, and recovery via `invoice.paid` mid-grace to confirm restoration cleared the marker. The bugs in delayed-settlement billing hide in the gaps between events, and Test Clocks are the only practical way to compress those gaps enough to exercise them deliberately.

## What I would change

**Make the grace window configurable per tier rather than a hardcoded 60 days.** The two-month window was the right call for the highest-value accounts, but a team tier and an individual tier do not necessarily warrant the same recovery runway. Moving `graceExpiresAt` derivation onto a per-plan setting would let the business tune recovery aggressiveness without a code change, and would make the cron's behavior depend on data rather than a constant buried in a handler.

**Add a reconciliation job for stuck pending settlements.** Optimistic provisioning leaves a `settlementPending` flag that is cleared by `payment_intent.succeeded`. If that event is ever missed, a subscription stays pending-paid indefinitely while behaving as fully paid. A daily reconciliation that queries Stripe for the actual PaymentIntent status of any account that has been pending past the normal settlement window would catch dropped webhooks before they become silent revenue discrepancies, the same way a watchdog catches a background job that stopped reporting.

**Track the manual recovery outcome, not just the grace state.** Right now the system knows a subscription is in grace and knows when it exits, but it does not record why: whether ops recovered the payment, whether the customer fixed their bank account, or whether it expired into suspension. Capturing that outcome would turn the grace period from an operational stopgap into a dataset about why high-value ACH payments fail, which is exactly the kind of signal worth having before the next pricing change.
